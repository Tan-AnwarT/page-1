Home Techniques Training

Every fighter is different. Body type, height, weight, and natural athleticism all play a role in how you should train. There is no “one-size-fits-all” routine—your training should be realistic and built around your unique strengths and weaknesses. This section gives you proven examples and guidelines to help you create your own path.

Step 1: Assess Your Fitness

  • Endurance: Count max push-ups, squats, burpees in 1 minute.
  • Stamina: Time your 1 km run or try 3 minutes of jump rope.
  • Flexibility: Test splits, hamstring reach, and shoulder mobility.
  • Strength baseline: How many pull-ups or dips can you do with proper form?

Step 2: Create Your Workout Plan

  • Strength: Push-ups (standard, diamond, explosive), pull-ups, bodyweight squats, lunges, dumbbell rows.
  • Cardio: Jump rope, shadow boxing rounds, sprints, roadwork (long runs for endurance).
  • Explosiveness: Box jumps, plyometric push-ups, sprint intervals, medicine ball throws.
  • Flexibility: Dynamic stretches (before training), static stretches (after training), yoga-based mobility drills.

Example weekly split:
- Day 1: Strength + Technique drills
- Day 2: Cardio + Sparring rounds
- Day 3: Explosive drills + Bag work
- Day 4: Active recovery (stretching, mobility)
- Day 5: Full mix (strength + cardio + sparring)

Step 3: Train Consistently

  • Schedule 3–5 training days per week—consistency beats intensity.
  • Keep a training journal to track reps, rounds, and improvements.
  • Gradually increase intensity (progressive overload) to avoid injury.
  • Balance recovery—your body grows during rest, not just in the gym.

Step 4: Stay Motivated & Connected

Training is tough. Motivation fades, but discipline keeps you going. Surround yourself with like-minded fighters for accountability, feedback, and encouragement.

  • Join our Discord community to share progress and get guidance.
  • Find a sparring partner or local training group.
  • Reward small milestones—new gloves, rest day, or posting your progress.
  • Remember: the best fighters aren’t the strongest, but the most consistent.
